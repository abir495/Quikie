MODULE II ENGLISH IN THE BRITISH ISLES 450-1500 (Weightage: 25%)

2.1 Language Situation in Britain 450-1500 CE « Background

This period is the most linguistically turbulent in English history. It saw three distinct waves of extemal
influence — Scandinavian, then Norman French — layered onto the original Anglo-Saxon base, while
Latin continued as the language of the Church throughout. By 1500 CE, English had transformed almost
beyond recognition from the Old English of 450 CE.

2.2 Foreign Influences on Old English *** HIGH

* EXAM TIP
Examiner favourite for both Q1 and Q2. Know all three waves: Celtic (minimal), Latin (two waves: Roman +
Christian), and Scandinavian (Viking Age). Give word examples for each.

Wave 1 — Celtic Influence on Old English
As discussed in Module |, Celtic influence on Old English was minimal. The Anglo-Saxon invaders

displaced Celtic speakers rather than absorbing them. Place names and a handful of common words
(brock, crag, loch) are the main legacy. Celtic influence on Old English grammar was effectively zero.

Wave 2 — Latin Influence (Two Sub-Waves)

Latin influenced Old English in two distinct historical moments, and it is important to distinguish them:

(a) Continental Latin / Roman Period Borrowings
As discussed in 1.5, these entered before the Anglo-Saxons reached Britain. Words for material culture:
wine, cheese, street, wall, cup, dish, mile, pound.

(b) Christian/Ecclesiastical Latin (6th—10th centuries)

In 597 CE, Pope Gregory the Great sent St Augustine of Canterbury to convert the Anglo-Saxons to
Christianity. With Christianity came an enormous wave of Latin vocabulary — the language of the Church,
learning, and manuscripts. This is sometimes called the second wave of Latin into English.

+ EXAMPLES

Ecclesiastical Latin borrowings: angel, candle, hymn, mass, monk, nun, priest, school, disciple, epistle, paradise,
temple, font, altar, psalm, minister, pope, creed, martyr, deacon, apostle, pilgrim. Also educational terms: grammar,
meter, verse.

Some of these words entered through Greek and were then Latinised before reaching English — words
like 'church' (Greek kyriakon), 'bishop' (Greek episkopos), and ‘devil’ (Greek diabolos). The Christian Latin
wave represents a massive enrichment of the English lexicon, particularly in the domains of religion,
leaming, and administration.

Wave 3 — Scandinavian / Old Norse Influence (Viking Age) *** HIGH

nearly three centuries. Danish and Norwegian Vikings first raided, then settled large parts of northem and

eastern England — the area that came to be called the Danelaw (formally established by the Treaty of
Wedmore, 878 CE, between King Alfred the Great and the Danish leader Guthrum).

The Danelaw covered roughly the area north and east of a line from London to Chester. Within this
territory, Old Norse and Old English coexisted and mixed intimately — the two languages were mutually
intelligible enough that significant borrowing in both directions occurred.

What Old Norse Gave English

The Scandinavian contribution is remarkable because it affected not just vocabulary but core grammar —
pronouns, prepositions, and common everyday words. This is unusual — grammatical elements are
almost never borrowed between languages unless contact is extremely deep and prolonged.

+ EXAMPLES

Old Norse borrowings — Pronouns: they, them, their (these replaced OE hie, him, hiera). Everyday words: sky, egg,
cake, take, call, want, get, give, birth, husband, sister, window, wrong, ugly, happy, loose, low, flat, ill, weak. Verbs:
die, ride (in the Norse sense), crawl, gasp, glitter, scare. Place names ending in -by (Grimsby, Derby, Whitby),
-thorp (Scunthorpe), -toft (Lowestoft), -ness (Skegness).

@ KEY NOTE

The replacement of the Old English 3rd-person plural pronouns (hie/him/hiera) with Norse they/them/their is hugely
significant — even the most basic function words were exchanged. This shows how deeply integrated Scandinavian
settlers were in daily Anglo-Saxon life.

2.3 Characteristics of Old English *** HIGH

* EXAM TIP
Very likely to appear as Q2 (diagram + long answer). Draw a mind-map with five branches: Compound Words,
Strong Verbs, Word Order, Winchester Vocabulary, Stress Pattern.

What is Old English?

Old English (OE), also called Anglo-Saxon, was spoken from approximately 450 CE to 1100 CE. It
descended from the West Germanic dialects of the Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and Frisians who migrated to
Britain from the 5th century onward. OE looks almost like a foreign language to modern eyes — it has
elaborate grammatical inflections, a different vocabulary, and different word order.

The most famous Old English text is the epic poem Beowulf (composed between 8th—10th centuries).
Other major texts include the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle and King Alfred's translations of Latin works.

(a) Compound Words

Old English was highly productive in creating compound words by joining two existing words to form a new
concept. This is called compounding. OE poetry especially favoured kennings — poetic compounds
used as metaphorical descriptors.

+ EXAMPLES

Compounds: godspell (god + spell = good news — Gospel), woruldcyning (world-king), tungolcreeft (star-craft =
astronomy), heofenweard (heaven-guardian = God). Kennings in Beowulf: hronrad (whale-road = sea), beadoleoma
(battle-light = sword), goldwine (gold-friend = generous lord).

(b) Strong Verbs

OE inherited from Germanic a system of strong verbs that changed their vowel in the past tense rather
than adding an -ed ending. This is called ablaut or vowel gradation. Many modern English ‘irregular verbs’
are survivals of this OE strong verb system. There were seven classes of strong verbs in OE, each with a
distinct ablaut pattern.

+ EXAMPLES

Strong verbs surviving into Modern English: sing/sang/sung, ring/rang/rung, drive/drove/driven, write/wrote/written,
speak/spoke/spoken, ride/rode/ridden, rise/rose/risen, break/broke/broken, freeze/froze/frozen, bind/bound/bound.
What grammarians erroneously call ‘irregular’ verbs are in fact following very regular ablaut patterns from Old
English — hence the course objective's statement about ‘irregular’ forms.

(c) Word Order

Modern English uses a fixed Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order to convey grammatical relationships. Old
English could vary its word order much more freely because it used inflectional endings (case endings)
to show grammatical function — not position. OE had four cases: Nominative, Accusative, Genitive, and
Dative, plus traces of an Instrumental case. In OE poetry and formal prose, verb-final (SOV) order was
common, reflecting the Germanic heritage.

As inflectional endings eroded (especially under Norse contact, which accelerated this process), English
had to compensate by fixing word order. The shift to SVO is one of the most fundamental changes in the
history of English grammar.

(d) Winchester Vocabulary

Winchester was the capital of the West Saxon kingdom and became the major centre of learning and
manuscript production in Anglo-Saxon England, especially under King Alfred the Great (r. 871-899 CE).
Alfred's ambitious programme of translating Latin works into English established a prestige variety of Old
English — Late West Saxon — centred on Winchester. This variety became the written standard for OE,
meaning that the OE texts we can read today primarily reflect the Winchester (West Saxon) dialect, not the
Anglian or Kentish dialects.

@ KEY NOTE

Winchester Vocabulary = the standardised literary vocabulary of Late West Saxon OE, associated with King Alfred's
court. It is not a separate dialect but a prestige written form. This matters because: the Norman Conquest of 1066
destroyed Winchester’s prestige, and the written standard had to be rebuilt — eventually in London English.

(e) Stress Patterns

In Old English, stress was placed on the root syllable — almost always the first syllable of a native word.
This is the Germanic stress pattern inherited from PIE. Prefixes in OE could be stressed (when they
modified nouns: andswaru = answer) or unstressed (when they modified verbs: andswarian = to answer).
This distinction between noun-stress and verb-stress survives in some Modern English pairs: PROtest
(noun) vs proTEST (verb); REcord vs reCORD.

2.4 Middle English (1066-1500) and the French Influence *** HIGH

* EXAM TIP

French influence after 1066 is another near-certain Q2 topic. Cover: Norman Conquest context, domains of French
vocabulary (law, food, fashion, government), the social stratification (French = elite, English = common), and the
eventual re-emergence of English.

The Norman Conquest (1066 CE)

On 14 October 1066, William the Conqueror (Duke of Normandy, France) defeated and killed King Harold
ll at the Battle of Hastings. This single event had more impact on the English language than any other in
its history. The Norman invaders spoke a dialect of Old French (Anglo-Norman French), and they
became the new ruling class of England — as nobles, clergy, lawyers, and administrators. For the next
300 years, England was effectively trilingual:

French Influence on Vocabulary — The Semantic Split

The most striking evidence of Norman French influence is the famous semantic split between
Anglo-Saxon words for animals and Norman French words for their meat. The Anglo-Saxon peasants who
tended the animals used OE words; the Norman nobles who ate the cooked meat used French words.
This split has survived to the present day:

French Influence on Pronunciation and Structure

Beyond vocabulary, French also affected English in subtler ways:

* Pronunciation: French introduced sounds and sound combinations new to English, such as the [v]
sound at the start of words (veal, vain, very — OE had no initial /v/), the [dll] sound (judge, gentle,
giant from OF), and stress on the final syllable in many borrowed words (hotel, café, machine).

* Spelling: French scribes introduced the spelling convention of for /kw/ (queen, instead of OE cwen),
for /t™l/ (church had been spelled cirice), and combinations.

* Suffixes: Many French suffixes became productive in English: -tion (nation, action), -ment
(government, movement), -ance/-ence (distance, evidence), -ity (dignity, nobility), -ous (famous,
curious), -al (royal, legal).

+ Word order: French helped consolidate Subject-Verb-Object order by reinforcing the erosion of OE
case endings.

The Re-emergence of English

By the mid-13th century, English began its comeback. Several events accelerated this:

+ In 1204, King John lost Normandy to the French king. Anglo-Norman nobles could no longer maintain
estates in both England and France and had to choose — most chose England, gradually shifting to
English.

* The Black Death (1348—49) killed nearly a third of England's population, disproportionately affecting
the Latin-educated clergy and French-speaking elite. English speakers filled administrative positions,
raising the language's social status.

+ In 1362, the Statute of Pleading decreed that court proceedings be conducted in English (not French).
In the same year, Parliament was opened in English for the first time.

+ Geoffrey Chaucer wrote The Canterbury Tales (c. 1387-1400) in the London dialect of Middle English
— this literary prestige accelerated English's re-establishment as a written standard.
