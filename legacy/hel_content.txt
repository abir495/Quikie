MODULE I   IN THE BEGINNING   (Weightage: 25%)
1.1 Introduction to the English Language ★ Background
English belongs to the West Germanic branch of the Indo-European language family. It is today spoken
as a first or second language by over 1.5 billion people worldwide, making it the most widely used
language in history. Yet English as we know it emerged only gradually over fifteen centuries of invasion,
migration, and cultural contact.
The study of how a language changes over time is called diachronic linguistics (from Greek dia =
through, chronos = time). This course adopts a diachronic approach, tracing English from its Germanic
roots through Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and into the contemporary global
language it has become.
■  KEY NOTE
Key term — DIACHRONIC: studying language change across time (contrast with SYNCHRONIC, which studies
language at a single point in time). Your course is explicitly diachronic.
1.2 Proto-Indo-European (PIE) Family of Languages ★★★ HIGH
★  EXAM TIP
This is ALREADY asked in your exam paper as Q2 (10 marks). You MUST be able to draw the full family tree and
discuss each major branch with at least one modern language example.
What is Proto-Indo-European?
Proto-Indo-European (PIE) is the reconstructed common ancestor of a large family of languages spoken
across Europe, Iran, and the Indian subcontinent. No written records of PIE exist — linguists reconstructed
it during the 19th century by comparing similarities across its daughter languages. Sir William Jones
famously observed in 1786 that Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin showed such deep structural similarities that
they must have sprung from a common source.
PIE is estimated to have been spoken roughly between 4000 and 3000 BCE on the Pontic–Caspian
steppe (modern Ukraine and southern Russia). As communities migrated westward into Europe and
southward into Iran and India, the language gradually differentiated into distinct branches.
Major Branches of the PIE Family
The PIE family is divided into several major branches. Each branch gave rise to a group of related modern
(and ancient) languages. Below are the nine principal branches you must know:

1. Indo-Iranian
The largest branch by number of speakers. Divided into the Indic sub-branch (Old Indo-Aryan → Sanskrit
→ Prakrits → Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, Marathi, Gujarati) and the Iranian sub-branch (Old Persian →
Avestan → Modern Persian/Farsi, Kurdish, Pashto). Key ancient text: the Rigveda (Sanskrit).
2. Hellenic (Greek)
Contains only Greek, but Greek has one of the longest continuously recorded literary traditions. Ancient
Greek (Homer, Plato) → Byzantine Greek → Modern Greek. Greek contributed enormously to English
through Latin and directly during the Renaissance (philosophy, medicine, science terms).
3. Italic (Romance)
Latin is the parent of all Romance languages. Latin → Vulgar Latin → French, Spanish, Portuguese,
Italian, Romanian. Latin itself (Classical and Ecclesiastical) poured into English in huge waves during
Roman occupation, Christian conversion, and Renaissance scholarship. French — a Romance language
— is the single biggest external influence on English vocabulary.
4. Germanic
The branch to which English belongs. Divided into: (a) West Germanic — English, German, Dutch,
Afrikaans, Frisian; (b) North Germanic — Swedish, Danish, Norwegian, Icelandic (Old Norse); (c) East
Germanic — Gothic (extinct). English descends from the West Germanic dialects spoken by the Angles,
Saxons, and Jutes.
5. Celtic
Languages of the pre-Roman inhabitants of Britain and Gaul. Divided into Goidelic (Irish Gaelic, Scottish
Gaelic, Manx) and Brythonic (Welsh, Cornish, Breton). Celtic was spoken in Britain before Latin and
Anglo-Saxon arrived. Despite early contact, Celtic left surprisingly few words in English — mainly place
names and river names.
6. Balto-Slavic
Eastern branch dividing into Baltic (Lithuanian, Latvian) and Slavic (Russian, Polish, Czech, Serbian,
Bulgarian). Lithuanian is considered extremely archaic and close to reconstructed PIE. These languages
had minimal direct influence on English.
7. Anatolian
Spoken in ancient Asia Minor (modern Turkey). Hittite is the most well-known Anatolian language and the
earliest attested IE language (c. 1700 BCE). All Anatolian languages are now extinct. Some scholars
argue PIE originated in Anatolia rather than the steppe.
8. Tocharian
Extinct languages discovered in manuscripts from western China (Tarim Basin). Surprising find because
Tocharian is more closely related to western IE languages (Celtic, Italic) than to its geographical
neighbours. All extinct.

1.3 Language Situation in Britain Before 450 CE — The Celts ★★ MEDIUM
★  EXAM TIP
Q1 in your actual exam paper asks about Celtic influence on English. This is a confirmed 5-mark topic. Write a crisp
50-word answer covering: place names, river names, and WHY Celtic influence was limited.
Who Were the Celts?
The Celts were the pre-Roman inhabitants of Britain. They arrived in two main waves around 800–600
BCE and 400 BCE. Celtic speakers occupied the entire island of Britain before Julius Caesar's
expeditionary raids (55–54 BCE) and before the full Roman conquest under Emperor Claudius in 43 CE.
The Celtic languages spoken in Britain were Brythonic Celtic — the ancestor of modern Welsh, Cornish,
and Breton. Goidelic Celtic (the ancestor of Irish and Scottish Gaelic) was spoken in Ireland.
Celtic Influence on English
Despite the Celts being the indigenous population of Britain, their linguistic influence on English is
surprisingly and famously minimal. Scholars estimate fewer than twenty commonly used words in Modern
English can be traced directly to Celtic. This is one of the most discussed puzzles in the history of English.
The main areas of Celtic influence are:
● Place names
The most significant Celtic legacy. Many British place names preserve Celtic roots: London (from Celtic
Londinium/Llyn-don), York (Eboracum), Kent, Devon, Cornwall, Dover.
● River and geographical names
River names are extremely conservative linguistically — they often survive conquest. Examples: Thames
(from Celtic Tamesa = 'dark river'), Avon (= 'river'), Exe, Esk, Axe, Usk, Wye, Severn, Trent. The Celtic
word 'avon' simply means river — hence River Avon literally means 'River River.'
● A few common words
Brock (badger), crag, bin, tor (high rocky peak), loch/lough (lake in Scottish/Irish English), bannock (a type
of bread). Some scholars include 'dun' (fort) which appears in place names like Doncaster and Dundee.
● Grammatical influence (disputed)
Some linguists (especially Markku Filppula and John Hines) argue that certain syntactic features of
English — like the progressive aspect ('I am doing') — may have Celtic origins, as this feature is far more
common in Celtic languages than in other Germanic ones. This remains debated.
Why Was Celtic Influence So Limited?
This is the key analytical question. Several reasons are proposed:
 The Anglo-Saxon invaders (from 449 CE onward) pushed the Celts westward and northward into
Wales, Cornwall, and Scotland. There was little sustained, equal social contact between the groups.
 The Celts were politically subordinated — conquered peoples rarely impose their language on
conquerors.
 Celtic-speaking communities became geographically isolated in the 'Celtic fringe' (Wales, Cornwall,
Scotland, Ireland), so cross-linguistic contact diminished rapidly.

1.4 Romanisation of Britain ★★ MEDIUM
Rome conquered Britain in 43 CE under Emperor Claudius. The Romans occupied most of England and
Wales for nearly 400 years (43–410 CE). During this period, Latin became the language of administration,
the army, commerce, and urban life. However, Roman Britain was essentially a bilingual society — Latin
above, Celtic below.
Latin's Impact During Roman Occupation
Latin words that entered British speech during this period survive in place names rather than in the general
English vocabulary. The suffix -chester / -caster / -cester (from Latin castra = military camp) appears in
Winchester, Manchester, Lancaster, Leicester, Colchester, Gloucester. The suffix -coln (from Latin colonia
= settlement) appears in Lincoln.
The Roman Withdrawal (410 CE)
In 410 CE, the Roman Emperor Honorius withdrew legions from Britain to defend Rome against the
Visigoths. This left Britain vulnerable, and within decades Anglo-Saxon tribes from the European mainland
began raiding and settling. Roman Latin had a limited direct legacy in spoken English — its major
contribution came indirectly through the Church (second wave of Latin) and through French (which
descended from Latin).
1.5 Continental Latin Borrowings (Before 450 CE) ★★ MEDIUM
Before the Anglo-Saxons ever set foot in Britain, their Germanic tribes on the continent were already in
contact with Rome through trade, military service, and cultural exchange. Words borrowed at this stage
are called Continental Latin borrowings — they entered the Germanic dialects that would eventually
become English, and so they feel deeply native.
✦  EXAMPLES
Continental Latin borrowings in Modern English: wine (Latin vinum), cheese (caseus), street (strata via = paved
road), wall (vallum), pound (pondus = weight), mile (mille passus = 1000 paces), butter (butyrum), cup (cupa), dish
(discus), kitchen (coquina), school (schola via Greek), bishop (episcopus via Greek).
These borrowings show that even before arriving in Britain, the Germanic peoples who became the
English were absorbing Roman material culture and its vocabulary. Words for new concepts (paved roads,
wine, coinage) were borrowed wholesale.

MODULE II   ENGLISH IN THE BRITISH ISLES 450–1500   (Weightage: 25%)
2.1 Language Situation in Britain 450–1500 CE ★ Background
This period is the most linguistically turbulent in English history. It saw three distinct waves of external
influence — Scandinavian, then Norman French — layered onto the original Anglo-Saxon base, while
Latin continued as the language of the Church throughout. By 1500 CE, English had transformed almost
beyond recognition from the Old English of 450 CE.
2.2 Foreign Influences on Old English ★★★ HIGH
★  EXAM TIP
Examiner favourite for both Q1 and Q2. Know all three waves: Celtic (minimal), Latin (two waves: Roman +
Christian), and Scandinavian (Viking Age). Give word examples for each.
Wave 1 — Celtic Influence on Old English
As discussed in Module I, Celtic influence on Old English was minimal. The Anglo-Saxon invaders
displaced Celtic speakers rather than absorbing them. Place names and a handful of common words
(brock, crag, loch) are the main legacy. Celtic influence on Old English grammar was effectively zero.
Wave 2 — Latin Influence (Two Sub-Waves)
Latin influenced Old English in two distinct historical moments, and it is important to distinguish them:
(a) Continental Latin / Roman Period Borrowings
As discussed in 1.5, these entered before the Anglo-Saxons reached Britain. Words for material culture:
wine, cheese, street, wall, cup, dish, mile, pound.
(b) Christian/Ecclesiastical Latin (6th–10th centuries)
In 597 CE, Pope Gregory the Great sent St Augustine of Canterbury to convert the Anglo-Saxons to
Christianity. With Christianity came an enormous wave of Latin vocabulary — the language of the Church,
learning, and manuscripts. This is sometimes called the second wave of Latin into English.
✦  EXAMPLES
Ecclesiastical Latin borrowings: angel, candle, hymn, mass, monk, nun, priest, school, disciple, epistle, paradise,
temple, font, altar, psalm, minister, pope, creed, martyr, deacon, apostle, pilgrim. Also educational terms: grammar,
meter, verse.
Some of these words entered through Greek and were then Latinised before reaching English — words
like 'church' (Greek kyriakon), 'bishop' (Greek episkopos), and 'devil' (Greek diabolos). The Christian Latin
wave represents a massive enrichment of the English lexicon, particularly in the domains of religion,
learning, and administration.
Wave 3 — Scandinavian / Old Norse Influence (Viking Age) ★★★ HIGH
The Viking invasions began in 787 CE (first recorded raid on Lindisfarne, 793 CE) and continued for
nearly three centuries. Danish and Norwegian Vikings first raided, then settled large parts of northern and

eastern England — the area that came to be called the Danelaw (formally established by the Treaty of
Wedmore, 878 CE, between King Alfred the Great and the Danish leader Guthrum).
The Danelaw covered roughly the area north and east of a line from London to Chester. Within this
territory, Old Norse and Old English coexisted and mixed intimately — the two languages were mutually
intelligible enough that significant borrowing in both directions occurred.
What Old Norse Gave English
The Scandinavian contribution is remarkable because it affected not just vocabulary but core grammar —
pronouns, prepositions, and common everyday words. This is unusual — grammatical elements are
almost never borrowed between languages unless contact is extremely deep and prolonged.
✦  EXAMPLES
Old Norse borrowings — Pronouns: they, them, their (these replaced OE hie, him, hiera). Everyday words: sky, egg,
cake, take, call, want, get, give, birth, husband, sister, window, wrong, ugly, happy, loose, low, flat, ill, weak. Verbs:
die, ride (in the Norse sense), crawl, gasp, glitter, scare. Place names ending in -by (Grimsby, Derby, Whitby),
-thorp (Scunthorpe), -toft (Lowestoft), -ness (Skegness).
■  KEY NOTE
The replacement of the Old English 3rd-person plural pronouns (hie/him/hiera) with Norse they/them/their is hugely
significant — even the most basic function words were exchanged. This shows how deeply integrated Scandinavian
settlers were in daily Anglo-Saxon life.
2.3 Characteristics of Old English ★★★ HIGH
★  EXAM TIP
Very likely to appear as Q2 (diagram + long answer). Draw a mind-map with five branches: Compound Words,
Strong Verbs, Word Order, Winchester Vocabulary, Stress Pattern.
What is Old English?
Old English (OE), also called Anglo-Saxon, was spoken from approximately 450 CE to 1100 CE. It
descended from the West Germanic dialects of the Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and Frisians who migrated to
Britain from the 5th century onward. OE looks almost like a foreign language to modern eyes — it has
elaborate grammatical inflections, a different vocabulary, and different word order.
The most famous Old English text is the epic poem Beowulf (composed between 8th–10th centuries).
Other major texts include the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle and King Alfred's translations of Latin works.
(a) Compound Words
Old English was highly productive in creating compound words by joining two existing words to form a new
concept. This is called compounding. OE poetry especially favoured kennings — poetic compounds
used as metaphorical descriptors.

✦  EXAMPLES
Compounds: godspell (god + spell = good news → Gospel), woruldcyning (world-king), tungolcræft (star-craft =
astronomy), heofenweard (heaven-guardian = God). Kennings in Beowulf: hronrad (whale-road = sea), beadoleoma
(battle-light = sword), goldwine (gold-friend = generous lord).
(b) Strong Verbs
OE inherited from Germanic a system of strong verbs that changed their vowel in the past tense rather
than adding an -ed ending. This is called ablaut or vowel gradation. Many modern English 'irregular verbs'
are survivals of this OE strong verb system. There were seven classes of strong verbs in OE, each with a
distinct ablaut pattern.
✦  EXAMPLES
Strong verbs surviving into Modern English: sing/sang/sung, ring/rang/rung, drive/drove/driven, write/wrote/written,
speak/spoke/spoken, ride/rode/ridden, rise/rose/risen, break/broke/broken, freeze/froze/frozen, bind/bound/bound.
What grammarians erroneously call 'irregular' verbs are in fact following very regular ablaut patterns from Old
English — hence the course objective's statement about 'irregular' forms.
(c) Word Order
Modern English uses a fixed Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order to convey grammatical relationships. Old
English could vary its word order much more freely because it used inflectional endings (case endings)
to show grammatical function — not position. OE had four cases: Nominative, Accusative, Genitive, and
Dative, plus traces of an Instrumental case. In OE poetry and formal prose, verb-final (SOV) order was
common, reflecting the Germanic heritage.
As inflectional endings eroded (especially under Norse contact, which accelerated this process), English
had to compensate by fixing word order. The shift to SVO is one of the most fundamental changes in the
history of English grammar.
(d) Winchester Vocabulary
Winchester was the capital of the West Saxon kingdom and became the major centre of learning and
manuscript production in Anglo-Saxon England, especially under King Alfred the Great (r. 871–899 CE).
Alfred's ambitious programme of translating Latin works into English established a prestige variety of Old
English — Late West Saxon — centred on Winchester. This variety became the written standard for OE,
meaning that the OE texts we can read today primarily reflect the Winchester (West Saxon) dialect, not the
Anglian or Kentish dialects.
■  KEY NOTE
Winchester Vocabulary = the standardised literary vocabulary of Late West Saxon OE, associated with King Alfred's
court. It is not a separate dialect but a prestige written form. This matters because: the Norman Conquest of 1066
destroyed Winchester's prestige, and the written standard had to be rebuilt — eventually in London English.

(e) Stress Patterns
In Old English, stress was placed on the root syllable — almost always the first syllable of a native word.
This is the Germanic stress pattern inherited from PIE. Prefixes in OE could be stressed (when they
modified nouns: ándswaru = answer) or unstressed (when they modified verbs: andswárian = to answer).
This distinction between noun-stress and verb-stress survives in some Modern English pairs: PROtest
(noun) vs proTEST (verb); REcord vs reCORD.

2.4 Middle English (1066–1500) and the French Influence ★★★ HIGH
★  EXAM TIP
French influence after 1066 is another near-certain Q2 topic. Cover: Norman Conquest context, domains of French

The Norman Conquest (1066 CE)
On 14 October 1066, William the Conqueror (Duke of Normandy, France) defeated and killed King Harold
II at the Battle of Hastings. This single event had more impact on the English language than any other in
its history. The Norman invaders spoke a dialect of Old French (Anglo-Norman French), and they
became the new ruling class of England — as nobles, clergy, lawyers, and administrators. For the next
300 years, England was effectively trilingual:

French Influence on Pronunciation and Structure
Beyond vocabulary, French also affected English in subtler ways:
 Pronunciation: French introduced sounds and sound combinations new to English, such as the [v]
sound at the start of words (veal, vain, very — OE had no initial /v/), the [d■] sound (judge, gentle,
giant from OF), and stress on the final syllable in many borrowed words (hotel, café, machine).
 Spelling: French scribes introduced the spelling convention of for /kw/ (queen, instead of OE cwen),
for /t■/ (church had been spelled cirice), and combinations.
 Suffixes: Many French suffixes became productive in English: -tion (nation, action), -ment
(government, movement), -ance/-ence (distance, evidence), -ity (dignity, nobility), -ous (famous,
curious), -al (royal, legal).
 Word order: French helped consolidate Subject-Verb-Object order by reinforcing the erosion of OE
case endings.
The Re-emergence of English
By the mid-13th century, English began its comeback. Several events accelerated this:
 In 1204, King John lost Normandy to the French king. Anglo-Norman nobles could no longer maintain
estates in both England and France and had to choose — most chose England, gradually shifting to
English.
 The Black Death (1348–49) killed nearly a third of England's population, disproportionately affecting
the Latin-educated clergy and French-speaking elite. English speakers filled administrative positions,
raising the language's social status.
 In 1362, the Statute of Pleading decreed that court proceedings be conducted in English (not French).
In the same year, Parliament was opened in English for the first time.
 Geoffrey Chaucer wrote The Canterbury Tales (c. 1387–1400) in the London dialect of Middle English
— this literary prestige accelerated English's re-establishment as a written standard.

MODULE III   ENGLISH LANGUAGE 1500 – PRESENT   (Weightage: 25%)
3.1 Renaissance and the Resurgence of English ★★ MEDIUM
The period 1500–1700 is called Early Modern English. It saw English transition from a language
recovering from French domination into the vehicle of a world literary and scientific culture. Three major
forces drove this transformation: the Renaissance, the Reformation, and the development of printing.
Socio-Political Conditions for English's Resurgence
The following conditions must be known for Q2-type conceptual answers:
● The Tudor Monarchy (Henry VIII, Elizabeth I)
Royal patronage of English literature and a strong centralised state helped English become the language
of national identity. Henry VIII's break from Rome (1534) weakened Latin's ecclesiastical dominance.
● The Reformation
The Protestant Reformation (1517, Martin Luther; spreading to England under Henry VIII) challenged the
authority of the Latin-speaking Catholic Church. Reformers emphasised reading the Bible in vernacular
languages — this directly elevated English's status.
● Nationalism and the rise of the nation-state
In the 15th–16th centuries, European nations increasingly identified with their vernacular language as a
symbol of nationhood. Writing in English became a patriotic act.
● The decline of Latin as a universal language
As nation-states consolidated, Latin's role as the sole language of learning, law, and science weakened.
Scholars began writing in their native tongues.
● Overseas expansion
England's colonial and trading ventures from the 16th century onward spread English globally and brought
new vocabulary from contact languages.
3.2 Standardisation of English ★★★ HIGH
★  EXAM TIP
Standardisation is a major Q2 topic. Learn the four agents in sequence: Printing Press → Bible Translation →
Spelling Reformation → Dictionaries → Grammar Books. Know key names: Caxton (1476), Tyndale (NT 1526),
King James Bible (1611), Johnson (1755).
(a) The Printing Press — William Caxton (1476)
Johannes Gutenberg invented movable type printing in Europe around 1450. William Caxton introduced
the printing press to England in 1476, setting it up at Westminster. This was arguably the single most
important event for standardising English spelling and vocabulary.
Before printing, manuscripts were copied by hand, and scribes freely varied spelling according to dialect. A
single word might be spelled ten different ways in different manuscripts. Printing required choosing one

basis for printed English was essentially the London dialect (specifically the East Midlands dialect of the
London area), which already had prestige as the language of the capital, the court, and commerce.
✦  EXAMPLES
Caxton's first dated printed book in English: The Dictes or Sayengis of the Philosophres (1477). He also printed
Chaucer's Canterbury Tales (1478) and Malory's Morte d'Arthur (1485). Caxton's choices about spelling and
vocabulary had enormous standardising power because his press produced hundreds of identical copies — dialect
variation became increasingly visible as 'wrong.'
(b) Spelling Reformation
Printing fixed spelling, but the spelling it fixed was often inconsistent and did not reflect pronunciation.
Throughout the 16th–17th centuries, spelling reformers argued for a more phonetically regular system.
Key figures:
 John Hart (1551, 1569): Argued strenuously for spelling to match pronunciation. He proposed a
reformed alphabet.
 Richard Mulcaster (1582): His work Elementarie argued that custom and tradition should govern
spelling, not phonetic logic — a more conservative position that ultimately prevailed.
 William Bullokar (1580): Created a reformed spelling system with extra symbols.
Despite the reformers' efforts, English spelling was largely fixed by printers in the late 16th century,
preserving many inconsistencies that persist today (knight, though, through, cough).
(c) Inkhorn Terms ★★ MEDIUM
DEFINITION  ■  Inkhorn Terms
Overly learned, pedantic words borrowed directly from Latin or Greek, often unnecessarily, during the Renaissance.
Called 'inkhorn' because they were products of the scholar's inkhorn (ink container) — words of the study, not of
real life.
Renaissance scholars and writers deliberately enriched English vocabulary by coining new words from
Latin and Greek to fill gaps — especially for scientific, philosophical, and technical concepts. However, this
practice was controversial. Critics (called purists) argued that English was being corrupted by
unnecessary foreign words; defenders argued that English needed enrichment to become a vehicle for
learning equal to Latin.
The debate between Inkhorn controversy participants was essentially between linguistic nationalism
(use English words or coin English-based words) and cosmopolitanism (borrow freely from Latin and
Greek for precision).
✦  EXAMPLES
Inkhorn terms that survived: anachronism, education, exist, extol, frivolous, harass, illustrate, impede, mundane,
necessary, spurious, temperature, vacuum. Inkhorn terms that were rejected (now archaic): adminiculation
(assistance), deruncinate (to weed), fatiguate (to tire), illecebrous (alluring), obtestate (to call as witness),
suppeditiate (to supply). Thomas Wilson's Arte of Rhetorique (1553) mocked inkhorn terms satirically.

(d) Bible Translations ★★ MEDIUM
Bible translation into English was one of the most politically charged and culturally significant acts of the
Renaissance-Reformation period. The Latin Bible (the Vulgate, translated by St Jerome c. 400 CE) was
the Church's authoritative text. Translating it into English put scripture in the hands of ordinary people — a
radical, dangerous act in the eyes of the Catholic establishment.
John Wycliffe (c. 1382)
The first complete translation of the Bible into English, made from the Latin Vulgate. Produced by Wycliffe
and his followers (the Lollards). Wycliffe believed every Christian should be able to read the Bible. His
translation was handwritten (pre-printing) and declared heretical. After his death, his remains were
exhumed and burned.
William Tyndale (NT: 1526; Pentateuch: 1530)
Tyndale's translation of the New Testament from the original Greek was printed — making it the first
printed English NT. He was executed for heresy in 1536. His prose style (simple, rhythmic, vernacular)
profoundly shaped subsequent English Bible translation and, through it, English literary prose.
The Great Bible (1539)
The first authorised English Bible, commissioned by Henry VIII after the Reformation. Based largely on
Tyndale's and Coverdale's translations. Required to be placed in every church.
The Geneva Bible (1560)
Produced by Protestant exiles in Geneva. Included annotations (margin notes), was the first Bible to divide
text into numbered verses, and was the Bible used by Shakespeare and the Pilgrim Fathers. Very
influential on Elizabethan English.
The King James Bible (KJB) (1611)
Commissioned by King James I, translated by a committee of 54 scholars. Widely considered the greatest
work of English prose — its dignified, rhythmic style shaped the English language for centuries. Phrases
from the KJB are embedded in everyday English: 'the skin of my teeth', 'a thorn in the flesh', 'the apple of
his eye', 'feet of clay', 'the writing on the wall'.

4.1 English in the Contemporary World ★★★ HIGH
English today is the world's foremost global language — used in international diplomacy, science,
technology, aviation, popular culture, and the internet. Understanding how this came about and what it
means for the language itself is the concern of Module IV.
How English Became a World Language
 British colonialism (17th–20th centuries) spread English to North America, Australia, New Zealand,
South Asia, Africa, and the Caribbean.
 The Industrial Revolution (18th–19th centuries) gave Britain economic power; English became the
language of trade and industry.
 American economic and cultural dominance in the 20th century — especially after World War II —
further entrenched English globally through Hollywood, popular music, and later the internet.
 English became the primary language of scientific publication: over 80% of research papers globally
are now published in English.
 The internet solidified English's position: a disproportionate share of online content is in English.
Kachru's Three Circles of English ★★★ HIGH
★  EXAM TIP
Kachru's model is the most important diagram for Module IV. Practise drawing three concentric circles with correct
country examples for each. This is a very likely Q2 diagram question.
The most influential model for understanding the global spread of English is Braj Kachru's Three Circles
Model (1985):
Inner Circle
Countries where English is a native language — spoken as L1 (first language) by the majority population.
These countries set the 'norms' of English. Examples: UK, USA, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Ireland.
Outer Circle
Countries where English has been institutionalised as an important second language (L2) — often a
colonial legacy. English has official or semi-official status. Examples: India, Nigeria, Pakistan, Singapore,
Bangladesh, Ghana, Kenya, Malaysia, Philippines. India has the largest number of English speakers in the
outer circle.
Expanding Circle
Countries where English is learned as a foreign language (EFL), without any colonial history or official
status. The use of English is growing but it has no special status. Examples: China, Japan, South Korea,
Brazil, Russia, Germany, Indonesia, Egypt, Saudi Arabia.

4.2 Characteristics of Indian English ★★ MEDIUM
Indian English (IndE) is the variety of English used in India — a country where English has been spoken
for over 350 years (since the East India Company's establishment) and where it has developed distinct
phonological, lexical, and grammatical features. India has no single mother tongue — with 22 scheduled
languages and hundreds of dialects, English functions as a crucial link language.
Phonological Characteristics
 Retroflex consonants: Indian language speakers often pronounce /t/ and /d/ as retroflex sounds
(tongue curled back to palate), giving IndE its characteristic accent. So 'water' sounds like 'waTer' with
a retroflex T.
 No distinction between /v/ and /w/: Many Indian speakers pronounce both as a labio-dental
approximant (somewhere between the two), so 'vine' and 'wine' may sound similar.
 Syllable-timed rather than stress-timed: British English is stress-timed (stressed syllables come at
roughly regular intervals). Indian English is often syllable-timed (each syllable gets roughly equal time),
giving it a more 'staccato' rhythm.
 Aspiration differences: The aspirated/unaspirated distinction for stops differs from British norms.
 Monophthongs for British diphthongs: IndE speakers may pronounce 'late' as /let/ rather than /le■t/.
Grammatical Characteristics
 Tag question: 'isn't it?' used as a universal tag regardless of the verb ('She came, isn't it?' instead of
'She came, didn't she?'). Also 'no?' as a tag.
 Progressive aspect overuse: Using the present progressive for stative verbs: 'I am understanding the
problem' instead of 'I understand the problem.'
 Reduplication for emphasis: 'Do it now only,' 'He is always telling only.' The use of 'only' and 'itself' as
emphasizers.
 Article omission or unusual use: 'She is in hospital' is standard British, but IndE may omit or add
articles in non-standard ways influenced by substrate languages (Hindi, Tamil, etc.) which lack articles.
 Question formation: Direct question word order in indirect questions: 'I don't know where is he going.'

Indian English has a rich vocabulary of its own — words borrowed from Indian languages, semantic shifts,
and new coinages:

4.3 Characteristics of American English ★★ MEDIUM
American English (AmE) diverged from British English (BrE) over the 17th–19th centuries as
English-speaking colonists established communities in North America. The first permanent English
settlement was Jamestown, Virginia (1607). By the time of American independence (1776), an American
variety was already discernible.
Noah Webster and the Standardisation of American English
Noah Webster (1758–1843) is the key figure in American English standardisation. His American Spelling
Book (1783, nicknamed the Blue-Backed Speller) sold over 100 million copies and standardised American
spelling. His great American Dictionary of the English Language (1828) defined American English
independently of British norms. Webster deliberately Americanised spellings for reasons of nationalism,
logic, and simplicity:

Phonological Characteristics of American English
 Rhotic accent: AmE is rhotic — speakers pronounce /r/ in all positions, including word-finally and
before consonants (car, bird, further). Most BrE accents are non-rhotic (the /r/ is silent in these
positions). This is one of the clearest phonological divides.
 Flapping: In AmE, the /t/ between vowels is often pronounced as a flap (like a quick /d/): 'butter'
sounds like 'budder', 'water' like 'wader', 'better' like 'bedder.'
 Cot-caught merger: Many AmE speakers do not distinguish between the vowels in 'cot' and 'caught'
— both are pronounced the same.

Etymon — The original word from which a later word derives.
Inkhorn terms — Overly learned Latin/Greek coinages of the Renaissance, many of which failed to

Kenning — A poetic compound in OE (e.g. 'whale-road' = sea; 'battle-light' = sword).

Vernacular — The native or common language of a region or community (as opposed to Latin in medieval

West Saxon — The OE dialect of Wessex, which became the prestige written standard (Winchester
vocabulary).
World Englishes — The study of the many varieties of English spoken globally, each with its own
legitimate norms.
