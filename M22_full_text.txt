MODULE II: ENGLISH IN THE BRITISH ISLES 450–1500 CE (Weightage:
 25%)
 This period is the most linguistically turbulent in English history. It saw three distinct waves of external
influence — Scandinavian, then Norman French — layered onto the original Anglo-Saxon base, while
Latin continued as the language of the Church throughout. By 1500 CE, English had transformed almost
beyond recognition from the Old English of 450 CE.
2.2 Foreign Influences on Old English ★★★ HIGH
★ EXAM TIP: Examiner favourite for both Q1 and Q2. Know all three waves: Celtic (minimal), Latin (two
waves: Roman + Christian), and Scandinavian (Viking Age). Give word examples for each.
Wave 1 — Celtic Influence on Old English
As covered in Module I, Celtic influence on Old English was minimal. The Anglo-Saxon invaders displaced
Celtic speakers rather than absorbing them. Place names and a handful of common words (brock, crag,
loch) are the main legacy. Celtic influence on Old English grammar was effectively zero.
Wave 2 — Latin Influence (Two Sub-Waves)
Latin influenced Old English in two distinct historical moments. It is important to distinguish them:
(a) Continental Latin / Roman Period Borrowings
These entered before the Anglo-Saxons reached Britain. Words for material culture absorbed through
trade and contact with Rome on the European continent.
✦ EXAMPLES:
wine (vinum), cheese (caseus), street (strata via = paved road), wall (vallum), cup (cupa), dish (discus),
mile (mille passus), pound (pondus), kitchen (coquina), butter (butyrum).
(b) Christian / Ecclesiastical Latin (6th–10th centuries)
In 597 CE, Pope Gregory the Great sent St Augustine of Canterbury to convert the Anglo-Saxons to
Christianity. With Christianity came an enormous wave of Latin vocabulary — the language of the Church,
learning, and manuscripts. This is called the second wave of Latin into English.
✦ EXAMPLES — Ecclesiastical Latin borrowings:
angel, candle, hymn, mass, monk, nun, priest, school, disciple, epistle, paradise, temple, font, altar,
psalm, minister, pope, creed, martyr, deacon, apostle, pilgrim. Also educational terms: grammar,
meter, verse. Note: some of these entered through Greek then were Latinised — church (Greek kyriakon),
bishop (Greek episkopos), devil (Greek diabolos).
Wave 3 — Scandinavian / Old Norse Influence (Viking Age) ★★★ HIGH
The Viking invasions began in 787 CE (first recorded raid on Lindisfarne, 793 CE) and continued for nearly
three centuries. Danish and Norwegian Vikings first raided, then settled large parts of northern and eastern
England — the area called the Danelaw, formally established by the Treaty of Wedmore (878 CE)
between King Alfred the Great and the Danish leader Guthrum.

The Danelaw covered roughly the area north and east of a line from London to Chester. Within this
territory, Old Norse and Old English coexisted and mixed intimately. The two languages were mutually
intelligible enough that significant borrowing in both directions occurred.
What Old Norse Gave English
The Scandinavian contribution is remarkable because it affected not just vocabulary but core grammar —
pronouns, prepositions, and common everyday words. This is unusual because grammatical elements are
almost never borrowed between languages unless contact is extremely deep and prolonged.
✦ EXAMPLES — Old Norse borrowings:
Pronouns: they, them, their (replaced OE hie, him, hiera).
Everyday words: sky, egg, cake, take, call, want, get, give, birth, husband, sister, window, wrong, ugly,
happy, loose, low, flat, ill, weak.
Verbs: die, ride, crawl, gasp, glitter, scare.
Place names: endings in -by (Grimsby, Derby, Whitby), -thorp (Scunthorpe), -toft (Lowestoft), -ness
(Skegness).
■ KEY NOTE
The replacement of the Old English 3rd-person plural pronouns (hie/him/hiera) with Norse they/them/their
is hugely significant — even the most basic function words were exchanged. This shows how deeply
integrated Scandinavian settlers were in daily Anglo-Saxon life.
2.3 Characteristics of Old English ★★★ HIGH
★ EXAM TIP: Very likely to appear as Q2 (diagram + long answer). Draw a mind-map with five branches:
Compound Words, Strong Verbs, Word Order, Winchester Vocabulary, Stress Pattern.
Old English (OE), also called Anglo-Saxon, was spoken from approximately 450 CE to 1100 CE. It
descended from the West Germanic dialects of the Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and Frisians who migrated to
Britain from the 5th century onward. OE looks almost like a foreign language to modern eyes — it has
elaborate grammatical inflections, different vocabulary, and different word order. The most famous OE text
is the epic poem Beowulf (composed 8th–10th centuries). Other major texts include the Anglo-Saxon
Chronicle and King Alfred's translations of Latin works.
(a) Compound Words
Old English was highly productive in creating compound words by joining two existing words to form a new
concept. OE poetry especially favoured kennings — poetic compounds used as metaphorical descriptors.
✦ EXAMPLES:
Compounds: godspell (god + spell = good news → Gospel), woruldcyning (world-king), tungolcraeft
(star-craft = astronomy), heofenweard (heaven-guardian = God).
Kennings in Beowulf: hronrad (whale-road = sea), beadoleoma (battle-light = sword), goldwine
(gold-friend = generous lord).
(b) Strong Verbs (Ablaut)
OE inherited from Germanic a system of strong verbs that changed their vowel in the past tense rather
than adding an -ed ending. This is called ablaut or vowel gradation. There were seven classes of strong
verbs in OE, each with a distinct ablaut pattern. Many modern English 'irregular verbs' are survivals of this
system.
✦ EXAMPLES — Strong verbs surviving into Modern English:


--- PAGE 3 ---
sing/sang/sung · ring/rang/rung · drive/drove/driven · write/wrote/written · speak/spoke/spoken ·
ride/rode/ridden · rise/rose/risen · break/broke/broken · freeze/froze/frozen · bind/bound/bound.
■ KEY NOTE
What grammarians call 'irregular' verbs are in fact following very regular ablaut patterns from Old English.
These are not random exceptions — they are the oldest, most systematic verb class in the language.
(c) Word Order
Modern English uses a fixed Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order to convey grammatical relationships. Old
English could vary its word order much more freely because it used inflectional endings (case endings)
to show grammatical function — not position. OE had four cases: Nominative, Accusative, Genitive, and
Dative, plus traces of an Instrumental case. In OE poetry and formal prose, verb-final (SOV) order was
common, reflecting Germanic heritage.
As inflectional endings eroded (especially under Norse contact, which accelerated this process), English
had to compensate by fixing word order. The shift from free order → SVO is one of the most fundamental
changes in the history of English grammar.
(d) Winchester Vocabulary
Winchester was the capital of the West Saxon kingdom and became the major centre of learning and
manuscript production in Anglo-Saxon England, especially under King Alfred the Great (r. 871–899 CE).
Alfred's ambitious programme of translating Latin works into English established a prestige variety of Old
English — Late West Saxon — centred on Winchester.
This variety became the written standard for OE, meaning that the OE texts we can read today primarily
reflect the Winchester (West Saxon) dialect, not the Anglian or Kentish dialects.
■ KEY NOTE
Winchester Vocabulary = the standardised literary vocabulary of Late West Saxon OE, associated with
King Alfred's court. It is not a separate dialect but a prestige written form. The Norman Conquest of 1066
destroyed Winchester's prestige, and the written standard had to be rebuilt — eventually in London English.
(e) Stress Patterns
In Old English, stress was placed on the root syllable — almost always the first syllable of a native word.
This is the Germanic stress pattern inherited from PIE. Prefixes in OE could be stressed (when modifying
nouns: andswaru = answer) or unstressed (when modifying verbs: andswarian = to answer).
This distinction between noun-stress and verb-stress survives in some Modern English pairs: PROtest
(noun) vs proTEST (verb); REcord vs reCORD; PERmit vs perMIT.
2.4 Middle English (1066–1500) and the French Influence ★★★ HIGH
★ EXAM TIP: French influence after 1066 is a near-certain Q2 topic. Cover: Norman Conquest context,
domains of French vocabulary (law, food, fashion, government), social stratification (French = elite, English
= common), and the eventual re-emergence of English.
The Norman Conquest (1066 CE)
On 14 October 1066, William the Conqueror (Duke of Normandy, France) defeated and killed King Harold
II at the Battle of Hastings. This single event had more impact on the English language than any other in
its history. The Norman invaders spoke a dialect of Old French (Anglo-Norman French), and they became
the new ruling class of England. For the next 300 years, England was effectively trilingual:

The Semantic Split — Animal vs Meat
The most striking evidence of Norman French influence is the famous semantic split between
Anglo-Saxon words for animals and Norman French words for their meat. The Anglo-Saxon peasants who
tended the animals used OE words; the Norman nobles who ate the cooked meat used French words.
This split has survived to the present day:

French Influence on Pronunciation and Structure
 Pronunciation: French introduced sounds new to English — the [v] sound at the start of words (veal,
vain, very — OE had no initial /v/), the [dj] sound (judge, gentle, giant from OF), and stress on the final
syllable in many borrowed words (hotel, café, machine).
 Spelling: French scribes introduced <qu> for /kw/ (queen, instead of OE cwen), <ch> for /tsh/
(church had been spelled cirice), and <ou> combinations.
 Suffixes: Many French suffixes became productive in English: -tion (nation, action), -ment
(government, movement), -ance/-ence (distance, evidence), -ity (dignity, nobility), -ous (famous,
curious), -al (royal, legal).
 Word order: French helped consolidate Subject-Verb-Object order by reinforcing the erosion of OE
case endings.
The Re-emergence of English
By the mid-13th century, English began its comeback. Several events accelerated this:

Date
Event & Significance
1204 CE
King John lost Normandy to the French king. Anglo-Norman nobles could no longer maintain estates in both England and France and had to choose — most chose England, gradually shifting to English.
1348–49 CE
The Black Death killed nearly a third of England's population, disproportionately affecting the Latin-educated clergy and French-speaking elite. English speakers filled administrative positions, raising the language's social status.
1362 CE
The Statute of Pleading decreed that court proceedings be conducted in English (not French). Parliament was also opened in English for the first time.
c. 1387 CE
Geoffrey Chaucer wrote The Canterbury Tales in the London dialect of Middle English — this literary prestige accelerated English's re-establishment as a written standard.

Key Terms — Module II Glossary
Ablaut: Vowel alternation in strong verbs (sing/sang/sung). An inherited Indo-European feature; the
basis of OE 'strong verbs'.
Anglo-Norman: The dialect of Old French spoken by the Norman conquerors of England after 1066.
The elite language of Middle English England.
Danelaw: Region of northern/eastern England under Viking/Danish control after the Treaty of
Wedmore (878 CE). Zone of intense OE–Norse contact.
Kenning: A poetic compound in OE used as a metaphorical descriptor (e.g. 'whale-road' = sea;
'battle-light' = sword).
Semantic Split: The OE/OF division of meaning between animal name (OE) and its meat (OF):
cow/beef, pig/pork, sheep/mutton.
Strong Verbs: OE verbs that change root vowel for past tense (ablaut) — what we call 'irregular' verbs
in Modern English.

West Saxon: The OE dialect of Wessex, which became the prestige written standard (Winchester

Winchester Vocabulary: The standardised literary vocabulary of Late West Saxon OE, associated
